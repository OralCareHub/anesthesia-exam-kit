Sensory only → sup. orbital fissure
V2 Maxillary
Sensory only → foramen rotundum
V3 Mandibular
Mixed → foramen ovale

Key terminal branches you'll block in the exam

A · Plexus (Supraperiosteal) Anesthesia

Drop the needle just into the mucobuccal fold over the target tooth, bevel to bone.
Indication
1–2 teeth, mostly maxillary
Needle
Short
Depth
few mm
Volume
⅓–½ cartridge
Operator
9–10 o'clock
Onset
2–4 min
Contraindicated: multiple adjacent teeth (use a nerve block), acute inflammation/infection, dense bone over apices.
Tip: plexal = "near the apex" · troncular = "near the trunk".
V2 maxillary block

1 · PSA Block — Tuberosity Technique

Needle 45° upward + inward + backward, behind the zygomatic process, to 10–15 mm.
Nerve
PSA
Teeth covered
M3, M2, M1 (≠ MB root M1)
Soft tissue
Buccal gingiva of upper molars + post./lat. wall of max. sinus
Direction
up 45° + in + back
Depth
10–15 mm
Bone landmark
Zygomatic process of maxilla

Technique

  1. Disinfect mucosa (± topical).
  2. Bevel oriented to bone — do not sting periosteum.
  3. Aspirate mandatory (pterygoid plexus risk).
  4. Slow deposit while keeping bone contact.
Accidents: tuberosity hematoma (pterygoid plexus), pain (fast deposit / cold / thick needle), needle breakage.
Hematoma management: gauze in sup. fornix + compression with thenar surface.
V2 maxillary block

2 · Infraorbital Nerve Block

Palpate the infraorbital ridge first — your finger stays on the foramen the whole time.
Nerves
Infraorbital + ASA (± MSA)
Teeth covered
Incisors, canines, premolars
Soft tissue
Lower eyelid, side of nose, upper lip + part of max. sinus
Direction
parallel to long axis P1
Depth
1.5–2.5 cm
Volume
1.5–2 mL drawn

Technique

  1. Patient supine or seated. Topical anesthesia.
  2. Patient looks straight ahead — palpate the infraorbital ridge.
  3. Keep your finger on the foramen for the whole procedure.
  4. Retract cheek, insert ~0.5 cm from buccal surface, parallel to P1 axis.
  5. Advance toward the foramen; deposit a tiny test bolus to palpate the foramen painlessly.
  6. Aspirate → if negative, slow inject 1.5–2 mL.
V2 maxillary block

3 · Greater Palatine Block

Greater palatine foramen: 1 cm medial to upper M3, in the dihedral angle of palatine bone.
Nerve
Greater palatine
Territory
Posterior 2/3 hard palate + soft tissues
Direction
up + back + out (from opposite commissure)
Volume
0.2–0.3 mL
Position
Head extended, mouth wide open
Aspiration
<1% positive

Locating the foramen

  • Bone: 0.5 cm anterior to posterior edge of hard palate · 1 cm anterior to pterygoid hamulus · dihedral angle between alveolar process + horizontal plate of palatine bone.
  • Dental: 1 cm medial to upper M3 (or temporary M2 in children).
  • Soft tissue: 1 cm from gingival festoon of M3; mucoperiosteum looks paler / funnel-shaped.
Don't enter the duct. Once at the foramen, deposit only 0.2–0.3 mL — that's enough.
V2 maxillary block

4 · Nasopalatine Block (Scarpa)

Enter lateral to the papilla, from the side opposite to the one you want to block.
Nerve
Nasopalatine (Scarpa)
Territory
Anterior 1/3 hard palate
Direction
up + back + out toward target side
Duct entry
5–6 mm
Volume
0.2–0.3 mL
Aspiration
<1% positive

Locating the foramen

  • Bone: median palatine line, 1 cm behind the anterior alveolar ridge.
  • Soft tissue: 1 cm from gingival festoon, under the retroincisal papilla.
  • Dental: midline between the two upper central incisors.
Never sting the papilla — it is highly vascular and very painful. Massive bleeding possible.

Extraoral variants

  • Infiltration: needle into nostril at the nasal-floor / septum angle, 1.5 cm behind the orifice; deposit just under the nasal-floor mucosa; direction down + back.
  • Escat (topical): compress soaked in anesthetic placed 1.5 cm inside the nose for several minutes — exploits the superficial position of the NP nerve on the nasal floor.
V3 mandibular block

5 · Spix's Spine Technique (IAN + Lingual)

Lateral to ptg-mand fold, 1 cm above occlusal plane; syringe at opposite premolars, posterior + lateral + horizontal.
Nerves
IAN (+ mental + incisive) + Lingual
Teeth
All teeth on one hemiarch
Soft tissue
½ lip · lingual gingiva to midline · ant. 2/3 tongue + floor
Direction
posterior + lateral + horizontal
Depth
2–2.5 cm → withdraw −1 cm for lingual
Failure rate
15–20%

Direct technique (the one you'll perform)

  1. Disinfect mucosa (± topical).
  2. Puncture lateral to pterygomandibular fold, medial to temporal crest, 1 cm above occlusal plane.
  3. Syringe body resting on the opposite-side premolars.
  4. Advance horizontal + outward + posterior, 2–2.5 cm, maintaining bone contact.
  5. Aspirate → if negative, slow deposit ⇒ T1: IAN anesthetized.
  6. Withdraw needle 1 cm.
  7. Aspirate again → if negative, slow deposit ⇒ T2: Lingual anesthetized.

Indirect technique (alternative)

  1. Syringe parallel to the same quadrant; advance to bone contact at temporal crest.
  2. Move syringe to opposite side; advance to bone contact above mandibular foramen.
Facial palsy — if you fail bone contact, the needle passes posterior to the ramus and deposits into the parotid → CN VII anesthesia. Always confirm bone contact.
Other accidents: pain, needle breakage (no sudden direction change), hematoma, neuritis.
V3 mandibular block

6 · Mental Foramen Block

Foramen sits ~ between lower P1 & P2 — line linking supraorbital, infraorbital and mental foramina.
Nerves
Mental + incisive
Soft tissue
½ lower lip, chin, vestibular gingiva (foramen → midline)
Teeth (incisive)
P → I (incl. periodontium + bone)
Intraoral direction
↓ + forward + inward
Extraoral insertion
1.5–2 cm behind oral commissure
Extraoral direction
↓ + inward + forward

Where the foramen actually sits (dental %)

  • Between roots of lower P1 + P2 — ~50%
  • Past lower P2 — 25%
  • Past root of lower P1 — 20%
  • Distal to P2 — 25%
  • In edentate atrophy, migrates closer to (or onto) the ridge.

Soft-tissue landmarks (find without bone)

  • Vertical through the buccal commissure.
  • Vertical through the internal canthus of the eye.
  • 0.5 cm medial to the mid-pupillary line (patient looking ahead).
V3 mandibular block

7 · Lingual Nerve — Isolated Blocks

Lingual n. runs ~1 cm anterior + medial to IAN. Superficial under floor mucosa just distal to lower M3.
In practice the lingual nerve is anesthetized as part of the Spix block (T2). The two techniques below are used when only the lingual needs blocking.

A · Lingual block at M3 (paralingual fold)

Landmarks
Lower M3 · paralingual fold · mandibular angle
Insertion
Next to lower M3, ½ distance gingiva → base of tongue, anterior + medial to angle
Direction
↓ + outward + posterior
Depth
0.5 cm

B · Dan Theodorescu technique (canine / P2)

Landmarks
Canine / P2 · paralingual fold · lingual surface of mandible
Insertion
Angle between oral floor + lingual surface of mandible, next to canine/P2
Depth
0.5 cm
Surgical hazard: distal to lower M3, the lingual nerve lies superficial under the floor mucosa — easy to damage during dental/oral surgery.
V3 mandibular block

8 · Buccal Nerve Block

Insertion happens on the upper arch, not the lower — even though the territory is lower-arch buccal gingiva.
Nerve
Buccal (anterior division of V3)
Territory
Vestibular gingiva: lower M3 → mental foramen (M3, M2, M1, P1) + cheek mucosa
Direction
posterior + parallel to upper occlusal + lateral

A · Classical buccal block

  • Landmarks: Stensen duct papilla · anterior margin of mandibular ramus · maxillary occlusal plane.
  • Insertion: intersection of upper occlusal plane with anterior margin of ramus; distal + vestibular to upper M3; 1 cm posterior + inferior to Stensen duct.

B · Vestibular infiltration (simpler)

  • Insertion: vestibular mucosa, distal + vestibular to lower M3.
  • Direction: posterior + horizontal + parallel to lower occlusal plane.

E · 10 Technique Commandments (apply to every block)

  1. Disinfect mucosa (alcohol or CHX).
  2. ± topical anesthesia.
  3. Bevel oriented toward bone — prevents periosteum tearing and sub-periosteal deposit.
  4. Maintain bone contact when block is "against bone" (PSA, Spix); never sting periosteum.
  5. Aspirate mandatory before every deposit — repeat after any needle movement.
  6. Slow deposit (≥ 30 s per cartridge fraction) to avoid pain & systemic load.
  7. Never insert needle to the hub — leave ≥ 5 mm exposed for breakage retrieval.
  8. No sudden direction changes mid-shaft (breakage).
  9. Patient position: head straight or slightly extended, mouth half-open (PSA) or wide open (palatal, Spix).
  10. Document onset time, volume, side; check anesthesia depth before proceeding.

F · What to Revise the Night Before

  • The Spix technique front-to-back — landmarks, direction, depth, T1/T2 sequence, the parotid / facial-palsy accident.
  • Distinguish greater palatine vs nasopalatine (posterior 2/3 vs anterior 1/3 · entry-from-opposite-side rule for NP).
  • Tuberosity hematoma prevention & management (always aspirate).
  • Which teeth the PSA block misses → MB root of M1 (needs supplementation, often plexus / infraorbital MSA).
  • The two lingual-only techniques (M3 paralingual vs Dan Theodorescu) — both depth 0.5 cm.
  • Buccal block: insertion at the upper occlusal plane × anterior border of ramus, not on the lower arch.
  • Trigeminal foramina trio: rotundum (V2), ovale (V3), Meckel's cave (ganglion).
